## Overview

Time entry descriptions are the written explanations that accompany logged hours in timesheets and invoices. Well-written descriptions justify billing to clients, aid project management, and provide an audit trail. Poor descriptions lead to client disputes, reduced realization rates, and difficulty tracking project progress.

## Why Descriptions Matter

### For Clients
- Understand what they're paying for
- See value delivered
- Verify work was done
- Approve invoices confidently
- Track project progress

### For Service Providers
- Justify billing
- Reduce write-offs
- Demonstrate value
- Improve realization rates
- Defend against disputes

### For Project Management
- Track what was accomplished
- Identify scope creep
- Understand time allocation
- Improve future estimates
- Document project history

## Key Principles

### Be Specific
❌ "Worked on case"
✅ "Reviewed deposition transcripts (pages 45-120) and identified key testimony regarding contract breach"

### Include Actions and Results
❌ "Meeting"
✅ "Client conference call to discuss Q3 marketing strategy; aligned on budget allocation and timeline"

### Reference Documents/Deliverables
❌ "Research"
✅ "Legal research on Delaware corporate law re: merger procedures; drafted 5-page memo summarizing findings"

### Avoid Jargon When Billing Clients
❌ "Debugged API endpoint"
✅ "Fixed technical issue preventing payment processing from functioning correctly"

## Template Structures

### Action + Subject + Outcome
"Reviewed [document/item] and [what was done/decided]"
- "Reviewed draft purchase agreement and revised sections 3-7 regarding pricing terms"

### Meeting + Purpose + Decisions
"Conference with [who] regarding [topic]; [outcome/next steps]"
- "Conference with client regarding trademark application; approved design changes and filing strategy"

### Task + Deliverable
"[Action taken] resulting in [deliverable created]"
- "Analyzed competitor pricing data and created comparison spreadsheet with recommendations"

## Good Examples by Industry

### Legal
- "Telephone conference with opposing counsel re: discovery schedule; negotiated extension to Nov 15"
- "Drafted motion for summary judgment (15 pages) with supporting exhibits"
- "Legal research on contract formation requirements under Massachusetts law; updated case memo"

### Consulting
- "Analyzed customer churn data Q1-Q3 and identified top 3 root causes with recommended interventions"
- "Facilitated strategic planning workshop with executive team; documented 2026 priorities"
- "Reviewed financial model for accuracy and updated revenue projections based on new assumptions"

### Creative/Agency
- "Design revisions to homepage mockup based on client feedback from March 15 meeting"
- "Copywriting for email campaign (5 versions); implemented A/B testing recommendations"
- "Social media content creation: 10 Instagram posts with captions for product launch"

### IT/Development
- "Implemented user authentication system with password reset functionality"
- "Troubleshot and resolved database performance issues causing slow page loads"
- "Code review of shopping cart module; identified 3 bugs and submitted fixes"

### Accounting
- "Prepared quarterly sales tax return for NY, NJ, and CT (Q3 2025)"
- "Reconciled bank statements for operating account (October 2025); resolved 7 discrepancies"
- "Tax research regarding R&D credit eligibility for software development costs"

## Poor Examples to Avoid

### Too Vague
❌ "Email"
❌ "Phone call"
❌ "Work on project"
❌ "Various tasks"
❌ "Administrative work"

### Block Billing (Lumping Together)
❌ "Review documents, draft memo, research case law, conference with client" (4 hours)

Better: Separate entries:
- "Review contract documents and exhibits A-D" (1.5 hours)
- "Legal research on breach of contract remedies" (1.0 hours)
- "Draft memo summarizing findings" (1.0 hours)
- "Conference with client to discuss strategy" (0.5 hours)

### Too Much Detail
❌ "Opened email from client at 9:47am, read through 3-page attachment, made notes in margins, called paralegal to discuss at 10:15am for 12 minutes, then began drafting response starting with greeting..."

Better: "Reviewed client's 3-page proposal and drafted response addressing questions re: timeline and deliverables"

## Industry-Specific Considerations

### Legal - ABA Guidelines
- Use task-based codes when required
- Describe work, not expertise (avoid "experienced judgment")
- No double-billing for multitasking
- Redact confidential information if shared externally

### Consulting - Value Focus
- Emphasize outcomes and insights
- Frame work in terms of client benefit
- Quantify results when possible ("identified $200K cost savings")
- Connect to strategic objectives

### Agencies - Deliverable-Oriented
- Specify what was created/delivered
- Note revisions/rounds clearly
- Reference client feedback incorporated
- Link to project phases

## Common Pitfalls

### 1. Using Same Description Repeatedly
"Work on XYZ project" for every entry looks suspicious

### 2. Over-billing Admin Time
Client doesn't want to pay for "organizing files" or "updating time sheets"

### 3. Describing Learning Curve
"Learning how to use new software" - bill time to yourself, not client

### 4. Vague Meetings
"Meeting" tells client nothing; always include purpose and outcome

### 5. Inconsistent Detail Level
Some entries detailed, others vague - appears arbitrary

## Time-Saving Tips

### Create Templates
Save commonly used descriptions:
- "Reviewed and revised [document name]"
- "Conference with [person] regarding [topic]"
- "Research on [legal issue/topic]"

### Use Abbreviations Consistently
- "re:" for "regarding"
- "w/" for "with" (informal contexts only)
- "Q1" for "first quarter"
- Industry-standard acronyms

### Track While Working
Write descriptions as you work, not end of day:
- More accurate detail
- Better recall of what was accomplished
- Faster timesheet completion

### Voice-to-Text
Use dictation features in time tracking apps:
- Faster entry
- More natural phrasing
- Can dictate while still in context

## Compliance and Ethics

### Be Honest
- Describe actual work performed
- Don't inflate significance
- Accurately reflect time spent

### Maintain Confidentiality
- Redact sensitive details if sharing
- Use generic terms when appropriate
- Follow industry privacy rules

### Document Thoroughly
- Enough detail to defend if questioned
- Useful for future reference
- Aids in malpractice defense (legal)

## Client-Facing vs. Internal

### Internal (Team Use Only)
Can be more casual:
- "Fixed bug in payment module"
- "Updated designs per Slack feedback"

### Client-Facing (On Invoices)
More professional:
- "Resolved technical issue preventing payment processing"
- "Design revisions incorporating feedback from March 10 review"

## Review Process

### Before Submitting Timesheets
- Read each description as if you were the client
- Would you pay for this based on the description?
- Is it clear what was accomplished?
- Is there enough detail to justify the time?

### Manager Review
- Check for clarity and professionalism
- Ensure consistent quality
- Verify appropriate detail level
- Flag vague or questionable entries

## Tools That Help

### Features to Look For
- **Description templates**: Save common phrases
- **Previous entries**: Copy from similar past work
- **Smart suggestions**: AI-powered description help
- **Spell check**: Catch errors before billing
- **Character counters**: Ensure adequate detail

### Training
- Provide examples of good vs. poor descriptions
- Regular feedback on timesheet quality
- Share best descriptions as models
- Make it part of onboarding

## Impact on Realization

Studies show:
- Detailed descriptions: 15-20% higher realization rates
- Clear work product: Fewer client disputes
- Specific entries: Faster invoice approval
- Professional descriptions: Improved client trust
