## Overview

The Planning Fallacy is a cognitive bias discovered by psychologists Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky. It refers to the tendency for people to underestimate the time needed to complete tasks, even when they have experience with similar tasks taking longer than expected.

## The Research

Key findings:
- **Students estimated** thesis completion in 34 days on average
- **Actual completion** took 56 days on average
- **Only 30%** finished by their predicted date
- **Even pessimistic estimates** were too optimistic
- **Past experience** doesn't automatically correct the bias

## Why It Happens

### Optimism Bias
- Focus on ideal scenarios
- Discount potential obstacles
- Overconfidence in abilities
- Desire to please others with optimistic estimates

### Inside View
- Focus on specific task at hand
- Ignore base rates from similar past tasks
- Discount external factors
- Plan for best-case scenarios

### Singular Focus
- Consider the task in isolation
- Forget about competing demands
- Underestimate interruptions
- Ignore other commitments

## Consequences

**Personal:**
- Chronic lateness
- Overscheduled calendars
- Stress from impossible timelines
- Reputation for unreliability

**Professional:**
- Missed deadlines
- Budget overruns
- Client disappointment
- Team stress

**Project Management:**
- Failed projects
- Cost overruns
- Delayed launches
- Reduced quality (rushing)

## Mitigation Strategies

### 1. Reference Class Forecasting
- Look at similar past projects
- Use actual durations, not estimates
- Apply historical averages to new estimates
- Trust the data over intuition

### 2. Breaking Down Tasks
- Divide large tasks into smaller components
- Estimate each component separately
- Sum the components (usually more accurate)
- Account for integration time

### 3. Buffer Addition
- Add 25-50% buffer to estimates
- Use the **1.5x or 2x rule** (multiply estimate by 1.5 or 2)
- Plan for the 80th percentile, not average case
- Include "padding" for unknowns

### 4. Pre-Mortem Analysis
- Imagine the project has failed
- List reasons why it might fail
- Plan for those scenarios
- Adjust timeline accordingly

### 5. Outside View
- Consult people uninvolved in the project
- Ask how long similar projects took
- Consider industry standards
- Use third-party estimates

### 6. Time Tracking
- Track actual time spent on tasks
- Compare to original estimates
- Calculate your personal bias factor
- Apply correction factor to future estimates

### 7. Account for Everything
- Include meetings, emails, interruptions
- Plan for sick days, vacations
- Remember administrative overhead
- Account for context switching

## Practical Applications

### Individual Level
- Track estimates vs. actuals
- Calculate personal multiplier (actual/estimated)
- Apply multiplier to new estimates
- Review weekly time usage

### Team Level
- Use story points, not hours
- Measure team velocity
- Plan based on historical velocity
- Account for team overhead

### Project Level
- Add contingency buffers
- Use three-point estimates (optimistic/realistic/pessimistic)
- Include risk analysis in timeline
- Plan for 80th percentile completion

## The Antidote: Inside-Outside View

**Inside View** (what we naturally do):
- Focus on specific project
- Imagine best-case scenario
- Rely on intuition

**Outside View** (what works better):
- Look at similar projects
- Use base rates and statistics
- Apply historical data
- Trust the numbers

## Tools and Techniques

- **PERT estimation**: (Optimistic + 4×Realistic + Pessimistic) ÷ 6
- **Reference class forecasting**: Use data from similar past projects
- **Hofstadter's Law**: "It always takes longer than you expect, even when you take into account Hofstadter's Law"
- **Time tracking apps**: Build personal database of actual durations
