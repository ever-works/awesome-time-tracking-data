## Overview

Maker Schedule vs Manager Schedule is a time management framework articulated by Paul Graham that distinguishes between two fundamentally different ways of organizing time: the maker schedule (requiring long uninterrupted blocks) and the manager schedule (organized in hour-based slots for meetings).

## The Two Schedules

### Maker Schedule
**Who**: Programmers, writers, designers, researchers, creators

**Characteristics:**
- Operates in half-day or full-day blocks
- Single meeting can disrupt entire day
- Requires extended concentration periods
- Context-loading takes significant time
- Interruptions are very costly

**Ideal Structure:**
- 4+ hour uninterrupted blocks
- Minimal meetings, clustered together
- "No meeting" days protected
- Async communication preferred

### Manager Schedule
**Who**: Executives, project managers, coordinators, sales

**Characteristics:**
- Operates in 30-60 minute slots
- Day divided into meeting blocks
- Frequent context-switching expected
- Coordination and communication focused
- Interruptions are normal workflow

**Typical Structure:**
- Back-to-back meetings
- Short gaps for email/follow-up
- Reactive and responsive
- High meeting density

## The Conflict

Problems arise when:
- Managers schedule makers into hourly meeting slots
- "Quick 30-minute meeting" destroys maker's 4-hour block
- Makers viewed as uncooperative for declining meetings
- Manager assumes maker can "just resume" after meeting
- Organizations default to manager schedule for everyone

## Solutions

### For Makers
- Block calendar for maker time
- Cluster meetings on specific days
- Communicate schedule needs clearly
- Use "office hours" for accessibility
- Protect morning/afternoon blocks

### For Managers
- Respect maker time blocks
- Batch meetings with makers
- Use async communication when possible
- Schedule meetings at block boundaries (start/end of day)
- Ask before scheduling over blocked time

### For Organizations
- Establish "no meeting" days or times
- Default to async-first communication
- Train managers on maker schedule needs
- Create calendar blocking culture
- Measure meeting load and impact

## Hybrid Roles

Many people need both:
- **Split Days**: Maker time morning, manager time afternoon
- **Split Weeks**: Maker days + manager days
- **Protected Core**: Core maker hours protected, flexible edges

## Implementation

1. **Identify Mode**: Determine if role is primarily maker or manager
2. **Communicate**: Share schedule preferences with team
3. **Block Calendar**: Visibly protect maker time
4. **Batch Meetings**: Cluster manager activities together
5. **Respect Boundaries**: Honor others' schedule needs

## Cultural Impact

Organizations respecting both schedules see:
- Higher quality creative/technical output
- Better maker retention and satisfaction
- More efficient meetings (managers prepared)
- Improved async communication practices
- Reduced meeting overload

## Best Practices

- Make schedule preferences visible
- Use calendar blocking effectively
- Batch interruptions when possible
- Default to async for non-urgent matters
- Protect maker time as fiercely as meeting time
- Educate team on schedule differences
- Build flex time for emergencies
